Arc Browser Reinvents the Desktop Tab While Forgetting the Future of the Web (and Everything Else) is Mobile

The Browser Company Spent $5 Million on THIS?!?

July 25, 2023

If you’ve found your way to Pizza Emoji, chances are this isn’t your first website. Chances are, you know the difference between a website, a web browser, a search engine, and an operating system. This is pretty basic terminology; nobody here is impressed.

But I think most of us who blog, vlog, toot, DM, and whatever messaging on Signal is called would be surprised that the majority of the online population can’t clearly differentiate between Google.com, Google Chrome, and Googling — at least, that’s my impression of the US population after having spent the better part of the last decade in IT.

IT — that’s a whole ‘nother nightmare that we’re going to glance over, but from my time in the cubicle I’ve come to realize just how little the average person knows about the Internet-with-a-capital-I. It’s like this: There’s a guy on the beach in a full suit and tie, holding a little brass telescope, wondering where all this water is coming from. Meanwhile, you and I are marching past in our full SCUBA gear, ready to explore the coral and befriend an octopus.

But you know what? Hanging out on the beach can be a lot of fun. There’s nothing wrong with a beach BBQ and a little frisbee in the sand. Build a sandcastle. Watch a sunset. Build a bonfire (local ordinances permitting). Go skinny dipping with your special someone. And then get eaten by a shark. (Look, I just re-watched Jaws the other night, I’m still in shark mode.)

The Arc mobile browser is laughably, stupidly bad.

All of this is to say, like SCUBA diving, the Arc browser is difficult to learn, extremely strange, and requires a deep understanding of the web/browser/OS relationship. Arc is designed for only the most experienced web surfers (ah, see what I did there?).

But technically new and strange isn’t a deal breaker for most Applications on the computer. For Arc, the make or break isn’t the desktop browser, it’s the mobile browser. And — spoiler alert — it's laughably, stupidly bad.

Arc’s mobile browser in the App Store on launch day is listed as “Arc | Mobile Companion” — and yes, that’s a pipe character after the name. (Emoji shrug!) The “Mobile Companion,” like the name would imply, won’t work if you don’t have and use the Arc desktop browser. Worse, it feels like an afterthought, and as someone who relies on his phone as much as — if not some days more so — than his MacBook, the Arc browser is dead in the water to me.

When first launching Arc, be sure to mute the speakers. For some reason, they play a little song on first launch. I’m not joking. It’s all very 2003 vibes.

The annoying trend in apps these days is handing over your email address and creating a password when you first start the app, and Arc forces this dumb functionality onto the user when launching the desktop browser. Despite iCloud syncing not requiring a separate sign-in, Arc does — possibly for the day when they eventually launch on Windows and Android. If you haven’t signed up for the desktop version of Arc, the mobile version actually tells you to go onto your computer and download the browser. Yikes!

Once signed into the desktop browser, Arc prompts the user to complete a little onboarding and watch a video on how to use the browser — there are a lot of little obnoxious pop-ups during the first ten minutes with Arc as it tells you how to report bugs, how to find the archive, and repeatedly begging to make it the default browser.

I started by opening some tabs in the sidebar, called a “space” where pinned tabs can be saved. Nothing can be bookmarked — don’t you dare try — instead, just “pin” websites to the side. Now, before you go and spend a great deal of time on this, I have a little warning for you: there’s an import option, but there’s no export option. Your pinned tabs will never escape Arc. This is a one-way road, and you can never (easily) go back. The longer you use Arc, the more difficult leaving will be. And I’m 100% certain this bullshit is by design.

The Browser Company (the actual company name, lol) was given 5 million dollars to develop Arc and they couldn’t include an Export pinned tabs option. That’s a little sus.

Arc doesn't include an Export pinned tabs option. That’s a little sus.

Also, keep in mind while pinning tabs, “Favorite” pins show up only in the desktop browser — all of my Favorite pins on the mobile browser are missing. Maybe this is by some strange design choice or maybe this is a bug (despite the browser being in beta for a year or so) but I highly suggest just regular “pinning” tabs, not marking them as a Favorite tab.

Arc is built on Chromium, the “open source” version of Google Chrome that is mostly stripped of Google’s web integrations. Why Chromium? I guess they felt Internet Explorer just wasn’t cool enough? The Chromium settings page is badly integrated into the Arc UI, and navigating through and disabling all of Chromiums bloat is tiring.

On the mobile side, there aren’t any settings… On the “Person?” screen there’s a button to delete search history or sign out of the app, but not even an option to change the default search engine — hope you like Google!

After 12 hours (by default, or a maximum of 30 days — but you can’t disable this functionality entirely) any open tabs that aren’t pinned are wiped away and sent to the Archive, which is also where all of the closed tabs go when clicking the Trash icon. Nothing in Arc is deleted, despite the icon implying otherwise. Everything just goes to a buried “Archive” screen somewhere in the browser. So imagine opening and closing a few hundred tabs throughout the day, and then the next day all of the tabs that you were saving to read and reference the next day are suddenly gone, tossed away with all of the other tabs from yesterday. What a nightmare. I keep a lot of tabs open that I haven’t had a chance to read when I go to bed, and I expect them to be there the next day — if Safari was just willy-nilly closing my tabs 12 hours later — I don’t know about you, but I’d go insane.

How do you trust your browser to keep _any_ of your tabs if it’s closing them on a daily basis?

How do you trust your browser to keep _any_ of your tabs if it’s closing them on a daily basis? Open a few tabs with your morning coffee and they’re gone before you’re in bed. Insanity. Arc sells this feature as “starting fresh” — I call it “urge to kill, rising.”

Aside from the pinned tabs/no bookmarks allowed functionality, Arc’s big new thing to web browsing is called Boost, which allows anyone to edit the HTML/CSS of a site, save those changes, and keep and share those changes across future browsing sessions. Say, for example, you’d prefer Pizza Emoji’s background color to be red. Why not! Using Arc, create a Boost, find the body {background-color: #222;} property, and change #222 to “red” or “#FF0000” and save the Boost. Now, when returning to the site, the background will again be red, and there’s nothing I can do about it.

Arc has a galley of pre-made Boosts, so if someone isn’t handy at HTML/CSS editing, they can install some boosts for popular websites like Google, Gmail, YouTube, and other non-Google sites (definitely says something about Google that so many of their sites are getting modded and showcased by Arc). This is similar functionality found in StopTheMaddness for Safari and other browser extensions for Chrome and Firefox from years past (or just clicked Show Source in the browser and making a local change to the stylesheet). The new twist here is sharing the masterpiece as a feature.

Of course, none of this works on mobile — sites can’t even detect if the iPhone is in dark mode, so half of the sites I was visiting were just blinding me with white backgrounds — including every time I searched for anything because there is no way to change the default search!

I guess I'm really struggling here to understand how a company goes about trying to reinvent the web browser, spends hundreds of hours moving the tab bar from the top of the screen to the left side, and then completely forgets about the phone. Apple didn't become a three trillion dollar company by selling Macs. The iPhone is half of Apple's annual sales — and usually it's more than half. And this is just Apple. Google, Samsung, Motorola, the list of iPhone copycats is never ending, because the market is never ending. There's no desktop app for Instagram, or TikTok, or Bumble — you're lucky if you've got a shitty web version of the app to login to. The apps that are changing the world right now are all on mobile (and maybe VisionOS soon?). And the apps on both desktop and mobile — 1Password, Fantastical, Things, Bear, PCalc, Day One, Flighty, Poolsuite FM, CARROT Weather, Tot, and Ivory, to name just a few — are equal in functionality, design, and usefulness. In 2023, they absolutely have to be.

I don’t know if the way Safari and Chrome and Edge do tabs and bookmarks is the best way to do them — after thirty-two years, maybe it’s time to kick bookmarks to the curb? I’d need to spend a lot more time with Arc to decide. I feel like the sidebar of tabs in Arc is interesting, but the mobile "Companion App" is just _so bad_ that I can't use it — and if I can't sync my open tabs and bookmarks between devices, the whole system just falls apart.

From my time in IT, I'm going to confidently state that _most_ people — the people hanging out on the beach, playing in the waves, lounging on a beach chair — do not want Arc as their browser. They don’t want their open tabs getting washed away 12 hours after opening them; they don’t want to go searching through the settings to change that functionality; they don’t want to edit CSS to change the background color of a website; and they don’t want to sit at their laptop all day because their mobile web browser is a piece of shit.

I don’t know who thought giving The Browser Company 5 million dollars to build a desktop web browser in 2023 was a good idea, but if I was them I’d ask for a refund.
