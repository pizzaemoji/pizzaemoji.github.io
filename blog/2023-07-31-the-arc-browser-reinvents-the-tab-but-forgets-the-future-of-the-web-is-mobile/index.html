by Brandon Butler

The Arc Browser Reinvents the Tab but Forgets the Future of the Web (and Everything Else) is Mobile

Arc's mobile browser is very, very bad, when it needs to be very, very good

July 31, 2023

Way, way back in 2019, I began developing the visual layout for Pizza Emoji. It was always intended to be a website for my writing, and because I did the initial development on my Mac using BBEdit, I began designing for desktop browsers. But I knew the site to look good on mobile, so I designed with percents and relative units like “em”. When I first loaded the site on my iPhone, I was pleasantly surprised by the results: on both desktop and mobile, Pizza Emoji looked great.

At the end of the day, Pizza Emoji doesn’t care what OS or browser you’re using, because it’s built with indifference towards your device and screen. If you want to be a mobile first Pizza Emoji reader, you’ll have no trouble here.

And that’s because the future of the web is mobile. Or, maybe that future has already occurred. Laptops aren’t going anywhere, but the average consumer (and web user) has no use for them.

So when I downloaded the new Arc web browser from the App Store on my iPhone, (made by “The Browser Company” — their actual name) I was baffled at how completely featureless, barren, and badly designed it was. In fact, it’s such a goofy web browser that it requires the user to first download, sign in, and use Arc on MacOS before being at all functional.

But it’s not just the mobile browser that’s bad — the desktop Arc browser kind of sucks, too.

The Arc browser has been in development for the past year, only releasing to the public as version 1.0 a few days ago. I wanted to try it out, as The Verge‘s David Pierce has been singing Arc’s praises for months while using the invite-only beta. He’s warned it’s a little different and takes some getting used to, so I spent some time getting used to it.

Arc will ask you repeatedly to make it the default browser: Just say no!

The initial onboarding experience is really bad — starting with the weird music it plays when first opening the browser. Yes, it’s very 2003 vibes. Then it requires an email address and password (sigh). (If you aren’t using Apple’s Hide My Email with iCloud, I gotta say, you’re missing out on a great feature.) After registering (and deleting the email from iCloud) I was asked to “pick a color,” prompted to sign into Google services, and asked (repeatedly) to make Arc my default browser. (No, seriously, the prompt to “make default” wil just pop-up and cover half the screen while browsing, like one of those obnoxious “sign up for our newsletter” boxes.)

Arc is built on Google’s Chromium, so out of the box it’s slow and battery intensive. Why Chromium? Maybe they thought Internet Explorer just wasn’t cool enough. Obviously, I don’t think it’s the right browser engine to build a company around, but nobody asked me.

Once Arc finally opens to the main browser screen, there’s yet-more onboarding and multiple open tabs to explain how to use the browser. It’s a lot, not going to lie. Arc’s approach basically boils down to this, though: Bookmarks are out, pinned tabs are in. But pinned tabs are basically bookmarks, but unpinned tabs actually disappear from the browser after 12 hours, which sounds more like a bug than a feature. How do you trust your browser to keep any of your tabs if it’s closing them on a daily basis?

Arc is built on Google’s Chromium — why Chromium? Maybe they thought Internet Explorer just wasn’t cool enough.

The little sidebar section where tabs live is called a “space,” and spaces are like the home screen on the iPhone — swipe left or right to see other spaces with other sets of tabs. But it’s too easy to get lost in spaces and lose track of open tabs. Arc doesn’t make it obvious that more spaces can be created, and I don’t see any advantage to having a long single space of pinned tabs verses swiping sideways through multiple spaces.

At the very top of spaces live the “favorite” tabs, and these get a special layout and remain the same across all spaces — but while spaces sync to the Arc mobile browser and all pinned and unpinned tabs appear, the tabs pinned as favorites don’t appear, anywhere, at all on mobile. Favorites, to me, would be sites I check everyday, probably multiple times a day, whether at my desk, in line at the store, or sitting at the airport. Why the bookmar— sorry, tabs I’ve marked as favorites don’t appear on mobile is baffling.

Now, before you go and spend a great deal of time on this, I have a little warning for you: there’s an import option, but there’s no export option for your tabs. Your pinned tabs will never escape Arc. This is a one-way road, and you can never (easily) switch to another browser down the road. Yeah, this is some bullshit user hostile trap, and this supposedly super-progresive company making the internet better by constantly begging you to set their browser as the default is so confident in their product there is no export option. The longer Arc is used, the more difficult leaving will be. And I’m 100% certain this bullshit is by design.

Arc has a few more “features” but none of these work on mobile, and I’m already so over this browser and this company, but here we go: Arc has a built in notes “app” and something called an “easel,” which honestly reminds me of Powerpoint a little too much. I’m not sure what the purpose of the easel is, and I’m not sure why I’d want my notes app built into my browser — especially considering notes can’t be edited on the mobile browser! (And neither can easels but I guarantee no one is using those.) There’s also something called Boost, which actually changes the local cache of a website’s HTML or CSS to alter it’s appearance, which can then be shared with other Arc uses (all twelve of them). Browser extensions like StopTheMadness and Cascadea can do similar browser tricks, but Boost uses a GUI to make these changes. Boosts, of course, don’t transfer to mobile.

Arc’s mobile browser in the App Store on launch day is listed as “Arc | Mobile Companion” — and yes, that’s a pipe character after the name. (Emoji shrug!) The “Mobile Companion,” feels like an afterthought, lacking basic functionality (like allowing websites to detect the system’s dark mode setting or changing the default search engine) and missing features of the desktop browser (editing notes, Favorite tabs, creating spaces).

Apps on both desktop and mobile — 1Password, Fantastical, Things, Bear, Ulysses, PCalc, Day One, Poolsuite, CARROT Weather, and Ivory, to name just a few — are equal in functionality, design, and usefulness. In 2023, they absolutely have to be.

I guess I’m really struggling here to understand how a company goes about trying to reinvent the web browser, spends hundreds of hours moving the tab bar from the top of the screen to the side, and then completely forgets about mobile. Apple didn’t become a three trillion dollar company by selling Macs. The iPhone is half of Apple’s annual sales — and usually it’s more than half. Some of the most popular iOS apps, like Instagram, or TikTok, or Bumble, don’t evem have a desktop app — and sometimes there isn’t even a shitty web version. But apps on both desktop and mobile, like 1Password, Fantastical, Things, Bear, Ulysses, PCalc, Day One, Poolsuite, CARROT Weather, and Ivory, to name just a few, are equal in functionality, design, and usefulness. In 2023, they absolutely have to be.

I don’t know if the way Safari and Chrome and Edge do tabs and bookmarks is the best way to do them. Maybe after thirty years it’s time to kick bookmarks to the curb. But I do know that overlooking the importance of mobile isn’t right, and it will be the reason why Arc — and The Browser Company — fail.

Most people do not want the Arc browser. They don’t want their open tabs getting washed away 12 hours after opening them; they don’t want to edit CSS to change the background color of a website; and they don’t want to sit at their laptop all day because their mobile web browser is a piece of shit. People have wanted — and used — a mobile-first Internet for years now. The Browser Company was so focused on redesigning tabs that they didn’t notice we’re all now living in the future, with our phones.
