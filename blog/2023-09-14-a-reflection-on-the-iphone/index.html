by Brandon Butler

A Reflection on the iPhone

3GS, 4s, 5, 6, 7, X, 11, 12, 14, 15

September 14, 2023

As we wait to pre-order the iPhone 15 Pro Max, my tenth iPhone, I’m reflecting on all of the previous iPhone models I’ve owned over the years. Sure, this’ll be fun...

My first iOS device was actually the first generation iPod touch. I had just signed a new two-year contact for a subsidized Treo with Sprint, and didn’t have the money or interest in terminating the contract and switching to AT&T, so I picked up an iPod touch. I used the hell out of that little guy — my Treo was for calls and texts, and my iPod touch was for everything else. Before the iPod touch, I remember that Palm Treo’s web browser and the stripped down “baby web” — it was always a bit of a gamble if the website you were loading was going to work on the Treo’s shitty little WAP browser. And then I remember opening Mobile Safari (on Wifi) with my iPod touch and having whole websites load for the first time. It was like going from dial-up to DSL all over again. I remember jailbreaking it and installing Cydia, and customizing the home screen in ways Apple never allowed (and still doesn’t), and installing apps and games that were terrible but made iPhone OS so much more fun. I remember buying Angry Birds for 99¢ on the App Store and playing the hell out of that silly little game before in-app purchases ruined iOS gaming. Just a few days with the iPod touch and I knew the iPhone was the future — not just of cell phones, but computing.

I was counting down the days until my Sprint contract ended, and in the summer of 2009 I ordered the iPhone 3GS from AT&T. The 3GS was the iPhone I used longer than any other phone, and arguably the one that photographed some of my favorite memories in grainy 3 megapixel jpegs. It’s also the iPhone I pried open several times to remove dirt and hairs from between the screen and display — back then, they were separate components and dirt could get between them. The iPhone 3GS also has the distinction of being the first — and so far, only — iPhone I have ever dropped: I was out doing some long exposure night photography with my dSLR on an overpass in November. My hands were freezing and I went to slip my phone into my front pants pocket but I missed the pocket and was just sliding it against my leg. I let go and heard it rattle on the ground before I realized what had happened. That plastic little phone took a beating over the years, I gotta admit, but it held up strong. A little nick on the bottom corner was all the damage it suffered from the drop.

Back then, phones were still subsidized and locked to a contract, so when my contract was finally up I had to decide: order the 4s, or wait another year for the next phone? I decided to wait (in retrospect, a mistake) and kept that little 3GS in my pocket until the Fall of 2012.

The iPhone 5 was the first iPhone with a new screen size, and that was a big deal. The iPhone 5 was also incredibly fast — and the first Apple product I owned with an Apple SoC (the A6). It was the first iPhone I owned with a front facing camera, which provided me with minutes of selfie fun until I realized I never wanted to do that again. The iPhone 5 was also the first iPhone I took to Disneyland — a big deal at the time, and I spent weeks agonizing over how to keep it dry on Splash Mountain (spoiler: a little water didn’t hurt it), if I should put it in a case (I did, for five minutes, nearly dropped it on Main Street because of the goofy case, and never did cases again) and embarrassed myself in front of Mickey (we don’t talk about that). The iPhone 5 was also the first iPhone to use the Lightning connector.

Lightning was arguably the biggest of the changes, and actually upset a lot of people — despite being a clearly better designed cable, people who had invested in the 30-pin ecosystem (which was HUGE back then) were upset that Apple was changing the cable. I firmly believe this backlash is one reason Apple waited until they were forced to change to USB-C on the iPhone 15 by the EU this year — blaming government regulation isn’t a bad way to deflect anger (although I think they’re probably secretly happy to have a clean break from Lightning — it’s old and clearly has power and data limitations that USB-C can solve). Lightning was an incredible cable back in 2012, and a lot of what makes USB-C so good is due to Apple’s development of Lightning. But I digress.

My next phone was the iPhone 6 (regular size). I was incredibly jealous of everyone with an iPhone 5s and their dual-core 64-bit SoCs and Touch ID home buttons. I remember agonizing over which size to pre-order, and actually made some rough replicas out of LEGO bricks to get a feel for the size. I don’t have huge hands and felt the Plus was just too much phone — a decision I confirmed when I went to the Apple Store several weeks later to play with the display models. Apple Pay was the big new feature that excited me, and I remember using it for the first time to buy my friend’s dog some dog treats at Petco. I knew then that the days of carrying a wallet were coming to an end for me. The iPhone 6 moved with me to Southern California, where Siri and Apple Maps became my constant companions. The iPhone 6’s design is probably my least memorable or favorite, but it got the job done.

The Jet Black iPhone 7 was, for a long time, my favorite iPhone design. It was really stunning, although the back became a little micro-scratched up over time. Also, the Lightning port came loose on it and I had to open it up and stick a new Lightning port in — a surprisingly easy replacement considering I had not dissembled an iPhone in several years. The iPhone 7 was the first iPhone with multiple rear cameras, including a “standard” wide angle lens and a new “zoom” lens. Due to the addition of the second camera, the “camera bump” on the back doubled in size. We all thought that was a lot — ah, to be young.

The “other” big new iPhone 7 “feature” was the removal of the headphone jack, making it water- and dust-resistant. Apple also announced with the iPhone 7 the new AirPod line of wireless earbuds, and yeah, I bought some of those on a whim when visiting the Apple Store in Pasadena. The removal of the headphone jack didn’t bother me, personally — my earbud cords were always getting tangled into a knot, and I spent so much time and money on those dumb little sticks for winding up the cords and they still got themselves into knots. In my opinion, the fewer the cables, the better.

Apple forgot how to count the following year, releasing the iPhone 8 and the iPhone X (pronounced “ten”) simultaneously, while skipping the number 9 entirely. By this point, the iPhone 8 was the 11th iPhone and the iPhone X the 12th due to the silly ‘s’ numbering system. I believe this was the first year that customers enrolled in the iPhone Upgrade Program could trade up for the new model, and I took advantage of it by handing in my iPhone 7 and claiming an iPhone X. Did I miss my beautiful Jet Black finish? Of course. Did I love the full screen display and Face ID on the iPhone X even more? You betcha. Face ID was a big deal, and many iPhone competitors still don’t have a secure, biometric Face ID-like technology in their phones. Face ID isn’t just taking a photo of the user’s face: it’s scanning the whole face with thousands of tiny infrared dots to confirm the person is a real, three dimensional person and not a photo or mask-wearing look-alike. The iPhone X was also — by necessity — the first iPhone to introduce new gestures for unlocking the device (swipe up) due to the missing Home button. It was disappointing that the iPhone X was only available in two colors, especially after the smorgasbord of colors we had to choose from for the iPhone 7. I opted for the silver/white finish, which I really enjoyed for its modern and bright appearance.

Here’s a fun story from my iPhone X days: I decided to switch carriers, but the carrier switch also necessitated a switch of cellular bands. I stopped by an Apple Store, because I thought my iPhone X had the correct cellular band for the carrier — but it didn’t. I felt really dumb for not checking this before hand, and could have been in a real pickle, but the Apple Store rep said it wasn’t a problem, and replaced the whole freaking iPhone for free to get me onto the correct network. She even asked if I needed help backing up the phone, but I verified I had an iCloud backup from the night before. About twenty minutes later my new iPhone was activated with my new cellular plan and I just watched as iCloud restored everything to the phone. Sometimes, the world surprises you.

The XS — the iPhone with the most confusing name to read aloud (“ten S”) — I skipped, and instead waited for the iPhone 11. The 11 was the first iPhone to have Pro and non-Pro models of the phone, with the phones having different designs and size options, as well as different camera configurations. I opted for the iPhone 11 Pro, basically the midde-of-the-pack iPhone at the time. While the iPhone 11 had the standard wide and ultra wide lenses, the iPhone 11 Pro had three — count ‘em three — camera lenses, adding a telephoto lens as the third. I returned to Space Gray and was soon regretful of the decision. I really don’t like that Space Gray color — it’s giving off serious Corporate America cubicle vibes. I had considered Midnight Green but it was so dark it looked like a murky, cloudy gray. Probably should have stuck with the silver and white.

The iPhone 11 and 11 Pro were announced and released in September of 2019, the same month and year I started Pizza Emoji. They will also be receiving the iOS 17 update — in addition to the iPhone XS — despite being four years old (five for the XS). Apple may try really hard to sell you new phones every year, but they’re also really good at supporting their older devices with continuous feature and security updates for years and years beyond the purchase date.

The iPhone 12 Pro I’d planned to skip, but when I saw that Pacific Blue color, I knew I couldn’t pass that up. By this point I’d mastered the trade-in system, getting a new phone for basically half off each year (the perks of disposable income) and made the swap. I really, really liked that Pacific Blue, possibly more than the Jet Black from the iPhone 7. I wrote about that iPhone in great detail on Pizza Emoji a while back.

I did skip the iPhone 13 Pro, as I didn’t love that light blue coloring, and was happy with the iPhone 12 Pro. But I did upgrade last year to the iPhone 14 Pro Max, in Deep Purple. Last year I finally made the plunge to the Max, and now, of course, I’m stuck with it. After a few hours I had adjusted to the size and weight of the largest iPhone, but it took a little longer to get accustomed to the Deep Purple. It basically looks like a very dark gray in low light, but then in sunlight it really shines as purple. I’ve come to really like it, and I really hope Apple keeps experimenting with colors. I’ll never own anything in Space Gray ever again. Of course, the iPhone 14’s big new visual change was the Dynamic Island, which hasn’t really been that amazing, but it’s fine. When I’m hiking, I’m happy to know I have a satellite overhead that will save me if I get myself into slightly too much trouble. The cameras are great, Apple Silicon is great, battery life is amazing — at this point, the phones have become stable, reliable computing devices, something many YouTube and tech reviewers probably find boring, but I’m okay with that. I want reliability and trust in my phone. That’s the most important thing, and that’s what the iPhone provides.

I did end up with an unexpected ninth iPhone a few months ago: the iPhone 4s. I’ve always loved the design of these iPhones. The iPhone 4s was also very popular, selling over 1 million phones in the first 24 hours of availability. The iPhone 4s introduced Siri (for better or worse) and was the last Apple product announced in Steve Job’s lifetime — he died the following day. This was also my dad’s first iPhone, and when he died recently I found this old iPhone sitting in a drawer. I replaced the battery and the screen, and because he kept it in a case, the metal sides and the back of the phone are incredibly clean. I need access to a PC so I can downgrade it to iOS 6, and then I think it’ll make for a nice piece of modern art, maybe running a little photo slideshow? It is truly a thing of modern, industrial, human-designed beauty.

I’m planning to pre-order the iPhone 15 Pro Max, and it will be my tenth iPhone (11th if we’re counting the iPod touch, but I’m not). I still haven’t decided on the color. I’m really interested in trying out that new camera zoom, and I’m looking forward to my next trip where I bring just one single cable to recharge everything (except the watch, heavy sigh). Oh, and that Action Button is going to be a big, big deal.

I’m looking forward to the iPhone 15 Pro Max this year. I think, for most people, this is a great year to upgrade, and a great year to start a new three or four or five year upgrade cycle. I think the iPhone 15 Pro will be remembered like the iPhone X and the iPhone 4 as ushering in a new era of iPhone design, connectivity, and fun.
