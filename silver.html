The Power Cosmic is for Everyone

The internet has decided Julia Garner can’t be the Silver Surfer. She’s the Silver Surfer.

by Brandon Butler
July 14th, 2025

The new Fantastic Four movie hasn’t been released yet, but I keep thinking about it: the moment Julia Garner descended from the sky in the first trailer, silver-skinned and solemn, was beautiful. Stunning. Cinematic.

I adore Julia Garner. And when I saw her as the Herald of Galactus, it hit me: of all the superheroes they chose to draw essentially naked, they picked a glistening chrome dude surfing through the cosmos. The embodiment of overt, toxic masculinity packaged for little boys and grown men alike. I knew two things from that trailer: the Silver Surfer as a woman was powerful and celestial and right—and the backlash would be predictable and dumb.

Here’s the tell: the comics already handed the board to a woman. Shalla-Bal has surfed the cosmos. She’s been one of several Heralds of Galactus. The big guy goes through Surfers faster than Sony goes through Spider-Men. They’re not hiding behind source material accuracy because they care about the lore. They care about themselves: Cis. Straight. White. Men. And any deviation from that default is a personal attack on their very identity.

It starts with the usual whine from men who think a fictional alien needs a penis for the plot. The tough guys who recoil at women in boardrooms, classrooms, and voting booths. The little boys who scream vulgar insults in voice chat when they get owned by a girl. The politicians who only remember women’s sports exist when a trans woman wants to play.

So they start to organize. They review-bomb Captain Marvel so hard that Rotten Tomatoes changes how it counts audience scores. Iman Vellani’s portrayal of Ms. Marvel—a Pakistani-American teenager—receives widespread praise, with one reviewer saying she’s the “true spirit of Kamala Khan.” The series is review-bombed before a single episode airs by grown men. They scream “forced diversity” because the show wasn’t made for them, the heteronormative default. A year later The Marvels limps to the lowest box office in Marvel Studios’ history. That’s the part that should actually piss everyone off: a vocal minority convincing a studio that diversity is a financial liability.

The problem isn’t Garner (or Iman Vellani, or Brie Larson, or Scarlett Johansson, or or Xochitl Gomez, or Dominique Thorne). It’s not about source material. It’s bigger than fandom. It’s about control—who has it, and who’s allowed to be seen with it.

For decades, the superhero has been a fantasy designed for men, built around masculine ideals like stoicism, ego, dominance, and a heavy dose of unresolved mommy issues. When those power roles are given freely to someone else—a woman, a person of color, a queer person—it’s not seen as inclusion. It’s trespassing.

For some, characters like the Silver Surfer or Superman aren’t just comic book icons. They’re an identity. Symbols people tattoo on their bodies and stick on their cars. But when that symbol shifts—when a woman embodies that Power Cosmic and strikes that mythic pose—they take it as a threat to their status as the default. The white, masculine, heteronormative daydream they’ve built around these heroes no longer centers on them. So they lash out. Cry about the canon. Weaponize review aggregators. Find affirmation in their echo chambers of toxicity. And when Garner descends from the sky, wrapped in computer-generated silver, they won’t admit what really bothers them: that a woman is holding power—cosmic, cultural, political—and refusing to apologize for it.

But isn’t that the point of the genre? That anyone—regardless of body, origin, or backstory—can become something more? It’s funny how we can read the same comic book, watch the same movie, but where some want to share the story with everyone, others were never taught to share.

When asked about the so-called “fans” who think only a man should play the role, Garner simply said: “I’m just going to still do my job.” That’s the answer. She doesn’t owe them an argument. Cosmic, cultural, and narrative power is not inherent only to men—it’s a superpower that can be wielded by anyone. And people like Garner are proof that any one of us can put on the cape, strap on the web shooters, hold aloft our magic sword, and save the universe.
