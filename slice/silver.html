The Power Cosmic is for Everyone

Why the internet doesn’t like the new Silver Surfer.

by Brandon Butler
July 14th, 2025

The new Fantastic Four movie hasn’t released yet, but I keep thinking about that moment Julia Garner descends from the sky on her surf board in the first trailer. The Silver Surfer. For a superhero movie, it was stunningly cinematic.

When I saw Garner as the Herald of Galactus, I realized that of all the superheroes they essentially chose to draw naked, they picked a glistening chrome dude surfing through the cosmos. Marvel’s First Family has been missing from the MCU for too long. Now they’re home, and just in time for Doomsday. Watching that trailer, I knew two things: the Silver Surfer as a woman was powerful and celestial and beautiful—and the backlash of overt, toxic masculinity would be predictable and dumb.

It starts with the usual whine from men and boys who think a fictional alien needs a penis for the plot. Tough guys who recoil at women in boardrooms and voting booths. Little boys who scream vulgar insults in voice chat when they get owned by a girl. Politicians who suddenly care about women’s sports when a trans woman wants to play.

These “fans” aren’t hiding behind the accuracy of the source material because they care about the lore. Shalla-Bal has surfed the cosmos on Earth X. The MCU is not Earth-616. The problem they have with a woman as the Silver Surfer is the default has changed. They are no longer the focus: Cis. Straight. White. Men. And any deviation from that default is a personal attack on their very identity.

So they start to organize. They review-bomb Captain Marvel so hard that Rotten Tomatoes changes how it counts audience scores. Iman Vellani’s portrayal of Ms. Marvel—a Pakistani-American teenager—receives widespread praise, with one reviewer saying she’s the “true spirit of Kamala Khan.” The series about a teenage girl is review-bombed by grown men before a single episode airs. They scream “forced diversity” because the show wasn’t made for them, the heteronormative default. A year later The Marvels limps to the box office because—you guessed it—grown men couldn’t handle the vibes. That’s the part that should actually piss everyone off: a vocal minority convincing a studio that diversity is a financial liability.

The problem isn’t Garner (or Iman Vellani, or Brie Larson, or Scarlett Johansson, or or Xochitl Gomez, or Dominique Thorne). It’s not about source material. It’s bigger than fandom. It’s about control—who has it, and who’s allowed to be seen with it.

For decades, the superhero has been a fantasy designed for men, built around masculine ideals like stoicism, ego, dominance, and a heavy dose of unresolved mommy issues. When those power roles are given freely to someone else—a woman, a person of color, a queer person—it’s not seen as inclusion. It’s trespassing.

Characters like the Silver Surfer or Spider-Man are comic book icons. And for some, they're an identity. Symbols people tattoo on their bodies and stick on their cars. But when that symbol shifts—when a woman embodies that Power Cosmic and strikes that mythic pose—they take it as a threat to their status as the default. The white, masculine, heteronormative daydream they’ve built around these heroes no longer centers on them. So they lash out. Cry about the canon. Weaponize review aggregators. Find affirmation in their echo chambers of toxicity. And when Garner descends from the sky, wrapped in computer-generated silver, they won’t admit what really bothers them: that a woman is holding power—cosmic, cultural, political—and refusing to apologize for it.

But isn’t that the point of the genre? That anyone—regardless of body, origin, or backstory—can become something more? It’s funny how we can read the same comic book, watch the same movie, but where some want to share the story with everyone, others were never taught to share.

When asked about the so-called “fans” who think only a man should play the role, Garner simply said: “I’m just going to still do my job.” That’s the answer. She doesn’t owe them an argument. Cosmic, cultural, and narrative power is not inherent only to men—it’s a superpower that can be wielded by anyone. And people like Garner are proof that any one of us can put on the cape, strap on the web shooters, hold aloft our magic sword, and save the universe.
